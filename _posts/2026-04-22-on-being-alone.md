Chapter 1 is about being alone.

That sounds simple. It's not.

When I first started outlining the book, I thought this chapter would be the easy one — a warm-up, something to ease the reader in before the harder material. What I found instead was that almost everything I believed about solitude was slightly off. Not wrong exactly. Just imprecise in ways that mattered.

---

The chapter starts with a distinction I kept tripping over while researching: the difference between *physical solitude* and *existential aloneness*.

Physical solitude is just logistics. No one else is in the room. That part, honestly, isn't the scary part for most people.

Existential aloneness is something else. It's the quiet recognition that you experience the world through your own senses, make choices with your own mind, and face whatever comes through your own consciousness — regardless of who else is standing next to you. The psychiatrist Irvin Yalom calls this *existential isolation*, and he frames it as one of the fundamental conditions of being human. It can't be fixed by having people around. It just is.

Most people never sit with that fact long enough to get comfortable with it. There's always something to reach for — a phone, a conversation, a screen, a plan. And the more you reach, the worse you get at tolerating the silence. It works exactly like a muscle. Neglect it and it weakens.

---

The personal story I tell in this chapter is about riding the bus alone as a kid.

My parents were hands-off, so by elementary school I was hopping on buses by myself, no GPS, no one checking in. Most of the time it was fine. But occasionally I'd end up on a route where the middle section ran through streets I didn't recognize — same destination, just unfamiliar scenery in between. And every single time, I'd panic. Heart racing. Mind going blank. Staring out the window, looking away, staring again.

The destination hadn't changed. The route hadn't changed. Nothing had changed except that I couldn't locate myself in the moment — and that was enough to undo me completely.

I kept coming back to that image while writing this chapter because it's such a clean picture of what the fear actually is. It's not about where you're going. It's about not being able to confirm where you *are* — and having to hold yourself together without anyone else doing it for you.

---

Attachment theory has a term for what makes that so hard: a *secure base*.

The psychologist John Bowlby found that children who were responded to consistently — reliably, predictably — develop an internal sense of *I am safe* that they carry with them even when no one is around. They can venture into unfamiliar territory and not collapse, because they know how to come back to themselves.

Children whose early environment was less stable don't build that. As adults, they read solitude as threat. They reach for their phone at the first hint of silence. They feel vaguely wrong eating alone. An unscheduled weekend produces a low-grade dread that's hard to explain. And in relationships, they look to other people to answer the question *Am I okay?* — constantly, reflexively, in ways that eventually exhaust everyone involved.

The thing is, none of that is a character flaw. It's an undertrained skill. There's a real difference.

---

The chapter ends with four practices, ordered easiest to hardest.

Eating one meal alone without your phone. A 30-minute walk three times a week, no earbuds. Twenty minutes a day of being genuinely unreachable. And eventually: going somewhere by yourself on the weekend without telling anyone — no narrating it in real time, no posting after. Just a thing that happened, that only you know about.

That last one sounds small. It isn't. There's something that shifts when you stop needing to make an experience real by having someone else witness it. When it can just belong to you.

---

Writing this chapter took longer than I expected — not because the research was hard to find, but because I kept finding myself in it.

I've spent a lot of time in my life being technically alone but not actually sitting with aloneness. Filling it. Managing it. Getting through it. It wasn't until a few years ago, after a period where solitude stopped being a choice and became just the condition of things, that I started to understand the difference between enduring it and actually being okay in it.

That's what I was trying to put on the page. Not a how-to. More like: here's what this actually is, here's why it's hard, here's what it looks like when it starts to get easier.

The bus always got there. That's the whole thing, really.

Even when I had no idea where I was — every single time, it got me where I was going.
